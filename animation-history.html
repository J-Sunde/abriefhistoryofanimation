PRE-1895
The Persistence of Vision Era
c.1000 AD

Shadow Puppets & Magic Lanterns

Long before film, Chinese shadow puppetry and the "Magic Lantern" (laterna magica) projected painted glass images onto walls. Showmen would manually shift overlapping slides to suggest motion — a horse galloping, a ghost rising.

Wikipedia →
FIRST! 1832

The Phenakistoscope

Belgian physicist Joseph Plateau invented the phenakistoscope — a spinning cardboard disc with sequential drawings viewed through slits. It was the first device to create genuine the illusion of continuous movement, proving the principle of persistence of vision.

Wikipedia →
1834

The Zoetrope

William George Horner's "Wheel of the Devil" (later renamed Zoetrope) replaced the disc with a spinning drum. Strips of sequential drawings placed inside created looping animations. It became a hugely popular Victorian toy and laid the conceptual groundwork for cinema.

Wikipedia →
WOW! 1878

Muybridge's Galloping Horse

Eadweard Muybridge set up 12 cameras with trip-wires to photograph a galloping horse in sequence. His "The Horse in Motion" proved animals could be captured in sequential frames — and directly inspired Thomas Edison and the Lumière Brothers to invent cinema.

Wikipedia →

1895–1928
The Silent Film & First Cartoons Era
FIRST! 1908

Fantasmagorie — The First Cartoon

French caricaturist Émile Cohl created Fantasmagorie — widely considered the first true animated film. Two minutes of chalk-line figures morphing endlessly into each other. Cohl drew 700 individual frames, each one by hand on a chalk board, photographed on film.

Wikipedia →
1914

Winsor McCay's Gertie the Dinosaur

Winsor McCay's Gertie the Dinosaur introduced the idea that an animated character could have a personality. McCay would stand on stage alongside the film projection, appearing to give Gertie commands — she'd stomp, toss McCay into the air, and weep on cue.

Wikipedia →
1919

Felix the Cat — The First Star

Created by Pat Sullivan and Otto Messmer, Felix the Cat became the first animated character to achieve true cultural stardom. His bold graphic design — solid black on white — reproduced perfectly in newspapers and on merchandise. He was the most recognised fictional character on Earth in the 1920s.

Wikipedia →
NEW! 1928

Steamboat Willie & Mickey Mouse

Walt Disney's Steamboat Willie was the first animated film with fully synchronised sound. Mickey Mouse (voiced by Disney himself) whistled, played a cow's teeth like a xylophone, and danced to music locked frame-perfect to the audio. Animation would never be silent again.

Wikipedia →

1928–1966
Hollywood's Golden Age of Animation
WOW! 1937

Snow White — The First Feature

Walt Disney's Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs was the world's first feature-length cel-animated film. Hollywood called it "Disney's Folly" — certain it would bankrupt the studio. Instead it grossed the equivalent of $1 billion today and won an honorary Academy Award plus seven miniature Oscars.

Wikipedia →
1940

Fantasia — Animation Meets Music

Disney's Fantasia married classical orchestral music to abstract animation for the first time. Featuring Bach, Beethoven, and Stravinsky, it pushed animation towards fine art. The Sorcerer's Apprentice sequence, starring Mickey Mouse, became one of cinema's most iconic moments.

Wikipedia →
1930–1969

Looney Tunes — Warner Bros.' Rebellion

While Disney aimed for art, Warner Bros.' Looney Tunes aimed for laughs. Directors like Chuck Jones, Tex Avery, and Friz Freleng created Bugs Bunny, Daffy Duck, and Tweety — characters defined by anarchic energy and self-aware humour that broke the fourth wall decades before it was fashionable.

Wikipedia →
1940

Tom & Jerry — The Ultimate Chase

Created by William Hanna and Joseph Barbera at MGM, Tom and Jerry pioneered the perfectly choreographed slapstick chase. With almost no dialogue, their shorts relied entirely on timing, musical scoring, and physical comedy — influencing virtually every animated short that followed.

Wikipedia →

1960–1990
Television, Anime & The Global Spread
NEW! 1960

The Flintstones — Animation Goes Prime Time

Hanna-Barbera's The Flintstones was the first animated series to air in prime-time television. Its success opened the floodgates — Saturday morning cartoons became a cornerstone of American childhood throughout the 1960s–80s, with Scooby-Doo, Yogi Bear, and dozens of others following.

Wikipedia →
1963

Astro Boy — The Birth of Anime

Osamu Tezuka's Astro Boy (Tetsuwan Atom) debuted as Japan's first weekly animated TV series, establishing the visual vocabulary of anime — large expressive eyes, speed lines, emotional internal monologue. Tezuka's studio Mushi Production would spawn an entire industry.

Wikipedia →
ART! 1985

Studio Ghibli — Animation as Cinema

Hayao Miyazaki and Isao Takahata founded Studio Ghibli after the success of Nausicaä. Their first film, Castle in the Sky, established Ghibli's signature: hand-painted environmental detail, complex female protagonists, and a profound emotional restraint that treated animation as high art.

Wikipedia →
1989

The Simpsons — Animation for Adults

Matt Groening's The Simpsons, launched on Fox, proved animation wasn't just for children. Its satirical, self-referential humour and consistent character development influenced every adult animated show that followed — from South Park to Family Guy to BoJack Horseman.

Wikipedia →

"My films are not fantasy. The world itself is a fantasy — I'm just trying to draw it accurately." — Hayao Miyazaki

1995–NOW
The CGI Revolution & Digital Frontier
FIRST! 1995

Toy Story — The First CGI Feature

Pixar's Toy Story was the first entirely computer-generated feature film. John Lasseter's team at Pixar spent four years developing the software, the story, and the visual language of CGI animation from scratch. It grossed $362 million globally and transformed the entire industry overnight.

Wikipedia → Pixar →
🏆 2001

Spirited Away — Animation's Oscar Moment

Hayao Miyazaki's Spirited Away won the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature in 2003 — the only non-English animated film ever to do so. A ten-year-old girl navigating a spirit bathhouse, it remains the most critically acclaimed animated film ever made.

Wikipedia →
WOW! 2018

Spider-Verse — Reinventing the Look

Into the Spider-Verse shattered assumptions about what CGI animation could look like. Its hand-drawn comic-book aesthetic — Ben-Day dots, expressive outlines, deliberately misaligned layers — proved that animation's visual language could evolve beyond photorealism. It won the Oscar for Best Animated Feature.

Wikipedia →
NEW! 2023–

The AI Animation Revolution

Generative AI tools have begun to reshape every layer of animation production — from AI-assisted in-betweening (automatically generating frames between keyframes) to text-to-video systems that can create entire animated sequences from a written description. The democratisation of animation is accelerating radically.

Wikipedia →

WHAT COMES
NEXT?

We stand at the most radical inflection point in animation's history. For a century, a single animated minute required twelve drawings. Today, AI systems can generate that minute from a sentence of text. Tools once requiring studios of hundreds are now accessible to a single creator with a laptop. Yet the greatest animated works remain those driven by the irreplaceable human impulse that started everything: the desire to make still images breathe, to give feeling to fiction, to make the impossible move.



From Lascaux cave walls to generative AI — the story of animation is the story of humanity's refusal to accept that pictures should stay still.
